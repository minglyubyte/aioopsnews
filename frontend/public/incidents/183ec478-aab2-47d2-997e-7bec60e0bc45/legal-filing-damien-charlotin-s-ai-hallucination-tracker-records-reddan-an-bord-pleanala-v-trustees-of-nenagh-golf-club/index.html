Trustees of Nenagh Golf Club in court with Pro Se Litigant linked to alleged or found AI legal hallucination. Outcome: Application for Judicial Review Denied; Express Judicial Rebuke for Misuse of AI. Nature: Misrepresented: Legal Norm | Applicant claimed Regulation 23 required the landing area to be outlined in red and neighbouring properties/contours shown; court held Reg 23 imposes no such obligation and the application concerned only the roofed bays. || Misrepresented: Legal Norm | Applicant grounded the JR on Article 40.4 personal liberty/property rights; court found Article 40.4 irrelevant to planning JR and no constitutional breach by the Board was alleged or evidenced. || Misrepresented: Legal Norm | Applicant invoked reckless endangerment under s.13 to stray golf balls; court treated these criminal law assertions as irrelevant and unsupported in the planning JR context. || Misrepresented: Legal Norm | Applicant accused the architect of 'subordination to perjury', a term the court noted is unfamiliar in Irish jurisprudence and likely AI-sourced, and entirely inappropriate. || Fabricated: Exhibits & Submissions | Applicant asserted the architect’s reference to a trustee letter was false because no such letter existed; court noted a date error explanation and treated the allegation as unfounded. || Misrepresented: Other | Applicant claimed the practice area since 2018 was unauthorised/a commercial driving range; court, relying on the Board’s addendum report, found the use authorised within existing permissions and non-commercial. || Fabricated: Other | Applicant alleged a 'suspiciously high' cancer rate around the course tied to environmental breaches; court found no evidence and described the claims as sensational and fear-inducing.. AI UseJustice Nolan suspected that Reddan's submissions, especially references to "subornation to perjury" and Constitutional Article 40 rights, were AI-generated, exhibiting typical hallucination patterns (pseudo-legal concepts, inappropriate cut-and-paste fragments). Reddan did not admit using AI but relied on internet-sourced legal arguments that closely resembled LLM-style outputs.Hallucination DetailsInappropriate invocation of "subornation to perjury," a term foreign to Irish law. Constitutional and criminal law citations (Article 40, Non-Fatal Offences Against the Person Act) irrelevant to judicial review context. Assertions framed in hyperbolic, sensationalist terms without factual or legal basis. General incoherence of pleadings, consistent with AI-generated pseudo-legal textRuling/SanctionThe High Court refused leave to apply for judicial review on all nine grounds. While no formal financial sanction was imposed, Justice Nolan issued a sharp rebuke, highlighting the improper use of AI and warning against making scurrilous, unverified allegations in legal pleadings. The Court stressed that misuse of AI-generated material could itself amount to an abuse of the judicial process.
